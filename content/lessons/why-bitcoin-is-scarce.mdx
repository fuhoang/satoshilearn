---
title: Why Bitcoin Is Scarce (21 Million)
---

Bitcoin is scarce because the protocol sets a fixed supply limit of 21 million coins. New bitcoin can be created only through mining, and even that issuance follows a known schedule written into the system's rules. No board meeting or emergency policy decision can casually change it.

This matters because scarcity is one of the most important properties of money. If supply can expand quickly whenever demand rises or governments face pressure, holders are diluted. A hard supply limit gives users a very different relationship with the asset. They know the rules in advance.

Bitcoin's issuance also slows over time through events called halvings. Roughly every four years, the reward miners receive for adding new blocks is cut in half. That means the rate of new supply falls over time until no new bitcoin are issued beyond the cap.

Scarcity alone does not create value. Something can be scarce and still useless. But scarcity combined with demand, usefulness, and credibility can matter a great deal. Bitcoin's significance is that its scarcity is not based on a promise. It is based on transparent, verifiable protocol rules.

When people say Bitcoin is scarce, they do not just mean "there is not much of it." They mean the supply policy is predictable, visible, and difficult to alter. That makes Bitcoin very different from forms of money managed by central issuers.
