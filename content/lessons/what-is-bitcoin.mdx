---
title: What Is Bitcoin?
---

Bitcoin is a digital monetary network that allows people to own and transfer value without relying on a central bank, payment company, or government database to keep the ledger. It is both a protocol and an asset. The protocol defines the rules. The asset, bitcoin, is the unit that moves through the network.

Most digital money before Bitcoin depended on a central operator. If you sent money through a bank or app, that company updated its own records and decided which payments were valid. Bitcoin introduced a different model: a shared ledger maintained by a distributed network of participants following the same rules.

At the center of Bitcoin is a simple idea: digital scarcity. Digital information is usually easy to copy. If money on the internet could be copied freely, it would be useless. Bitcoin solves this by making the network agree on one valid history of ownership. That means users can verify who owns what without trusting one central server.

Bitcoin was introduced in 2008 in a paper published under the name Satoshi Nakamoto. In early 2009, the software went live. Since then, the network has operated continuously, enforced by open-source code, economic incentives, and a large number of independent participants around the world.

Who created Bitcoin? The honest answer is that nobody knows for certain who Satoshi Nakamoto really is. The name may belong to one person or a group of people. What matters more than the identity is what was created: a working system, open-source code, and a design that did not depend on its creator remaining in charge. Satoshi eventually stepped away, and Bitcoin continued without a founder controlling the network.

Bitcoin has a fixed supply cap of 21 million coins. New bitcoin can be issued only according to the protocol schedule. No company can decide to print more because demand rises. No single government can rewrite the monetary policy for everyone on the network. That supply predictability is one of Bitcoin's defining features.

People often describe Bitcoin in different ways depending on what they value most. Some see it as money. Some see it as digital gold. Some see it as a censorship-resistant payment rail. Some see it as a long-term savings technology. These views overlap, but they all point to the same core property: Bitcoin is open, scarce, and verifiable.

To use Bitcoin, you do not need permission from a bank. To verify Bitcoin, you do not need to trust a marketing claim. The system is designed so anyone can inspect the rules, run software, and check transactions for themselves. That is a major shift from traditional financial systems, where verification is often hidden behind institutions.

Bitcoin is also different from most other cryptocurrencies. It launched without a central company managing supply or marketing token demand. Its monetary policy is simple. Its purpose is narrow. It is not trying to do everything. That focus is one reason many people treat it differently from the rest of the crypto market.

Why does Bitcoin matter? It matters because it offers an alternative to monetary systems that depend heavily on trust in central issuers and intermediaries. Bitcoin gives people a way to hold an asset with known supply rules, transfer value globally, and verify the system independently. Even for people who never use it directly, Bitcoin matters because it reopens an important question: what should good money look like in the digital age?

It also matters because it changes the balance between users and institutions. In traditional finance, access, settlement, and custody are usually bundled together. In Bitcoin, they can be separated. A person can hold their own assets, verify the rules, and move value across borders without asking a central operator to approve the transaction. That is not just a technical change. It is a different model of ownership.

The clearest way to think about Bitcoin is this: it is a monetary network for the internet, built around transparency, scarcity, and user control. If the previous lesson explained what money is supposed to do, Bitcoin is one answer to how those monetary functions might work in a digital world.
