---
title: Wallets
---

A Bitcoin wallet does not store coins the way a leather wallet stores cash. Bitcoin lives on the network's shared ledger. What a wallet really manages is your keys, the cryptographic credentials that let you prove control over specific bitcoin and authorize spending.

This point matters because beginners often imagine bitcoin sitting "inside an app." That is not quite right. A wallet gives you access to your balance by managing the secret information needed to sign transactions. If you control those keys, you control the bitcoin associated with them.

There are two broad wallet models: custodial and non-custodial. In a custodial setup, another company holds the keys for you. This is common on exchanges. It can be convenient, but it means you are trusting someone else with your funds. In a non-custodial wallet, you hold the keys yourself. That gives you greater control, but also greater responsibility.

Private keys should be thought of like secret signing authority. Anyone with the key can move the funds. That is why wallet security matters so much. A wallet is not mainly about having a nice interface. It is about managing access safely.

Most modern wallets also generate a seed phrase, sometimes called a recovery phrase. This is usually a sequence of 12 or 24 words that can recreate your wallet if your device is lost or broken. The seed phrase is extremely important. If someone gains access to it, they can usually recover and spend your bitcoin. If you lose it and your device fails, you may lose access permanently.

Good wallet practice starts with a few simple rules. Do not share your seed phrase. Do not store it carelessly in screenshots or cloud notes. Do not type it into random websites. Do not trust support messages asking for it. A legitimate wallet or service should never need your seed phrase to "help" you.

Wallet choice depends on your situation. A beginner holding a small amount may start with a reputable mobile wallet to learn the basics. Someone storing larger amounts for the long term may prefer a hardware wallet, which keeps keys isolated from everyday internet-connected devices. The right tool depends on how much you hold, how often you transact, and how much responsibility you are prepared to take on.

The most important takeaway is this: wallets are not mainly about where bitcoin is. They are about who controls the keys. Once you understand that, self-custody, exchange risk, and backup practices all become much easier to reason about.
