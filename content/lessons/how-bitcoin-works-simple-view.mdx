---
title: How Bitcoin Works (Simple View)
---

At a high level, Bitcoin works by letting thousands of computers agree on a shared record of who owns what. When someone sends bitcoin, they are not moving a physical object. They are creating a transaction that updates the network's ledger according to the system's rules.

Each transaction says, in effect, "these coins were under my control, and now they belong to someone else." To make that claim valid, the sender uses a digital signature created with their private key. Other participants can verify that signature without knowing the private key itself.

Transactions do not become final instantly. First they spread across the network. Then miners gather valid transactions into a block. Once a block is added to Bitcoin's history, the network treats those transactions as confirmed. The more blocks built on top, the harder it becomes to reverse them.

So the simple view is this: users create transactions, the network verifies the rules, miners add transactions to blocks, and the shared ledger updates. No central clerk is required to keep the master record.

What makes the system interesting is that all of this happens in public. The rules are open, verification is shared, and no single company controls the ledger. That is the basic engine behind how Bitcoin works.
