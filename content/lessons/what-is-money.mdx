---
title: What Is Money?
---

Money is a tool people use to move value across time and between other people. At its simplest, money solves a coordination problem. Instead of trying to trade your exact good or service for someone else's exact good or service at the same moment, you can earn money from one person and spend it with another.

Before money, trade depended on barter. Barter works in small settings, but it breaks down quickly because both sides need a "double coincidence of wants." If you have bread and want shoes, you need to find a shoemaker who also wants bread right now. Money removes that friction by acting as a commonly accepted intermediate step.

Good money usually does three jobs. It acts as a medium of exchange, which means people can use it to buy and sell. It acts as a unit of account, which means prices can be quoted in it. It also acts as a store of value, which means people can save in it and expect it to hold usefulness over time.

Not every form of money does all three jobs equally well. Some things are easy to exchange but poor for saving. Others are good for saving but awkward to spend. Over time, societies tend to prefer money that has several useful properties at once: durability, portability, divisibility, recognizability, and scarcity.

Durability means money should not rot, rust, or disappear easily. Portability means it should be easy to move. Divisibility means it should be easy to break into smaller units. Recognizability means people can verify it. Scarcity means it should be difficult to create in unlimited amounts, because if supply can expand too easily, trust in the money weakens.

This last point matters most. If money can be created quickly and cheaply, holders are diluted. Their savings buy less over time. That is one reason people throughout history have moved toward forms of money that are harder to produce, harder to fake, and easier to verify.

Modern national currencies are widely accepted and convenient, but they depend on central institutions. Governments and central banks can increase supply, change rules, and influence interest rates. That does not mean they have no value. It means the system depends on trust in managers and policy decisions.

This leads directly to one of the main problems with traditional money. Most people save in a system where the rules can change above their heads. If supply expands too quickly, the value of savings can erode. If access to banks or payment rails is restricted, moving money becomes harder. If a currency is managed poorly, ordinary people bear the cost through inflation, instability, or capital controls.

Traditional money also relies on layers of intermediaries. Banks, payment processors, card networks, and states all sit between the user and final settlement. That can make the system efficient in normal times, but it also means permission, delays, fees, and counterparty risk are built in. For many people, the problem is not that the system never works. The problem is that the system works only while trusted institutions continue to operate as expected.

In short, traditional money is useful, but it is not neutral. It depends on human decision-making, political incentives, and centralized infrastructure. Once you see those limits clearly, it becomes easier to understand why people became interested in building a monetary system with harder rules and less dependence on trust.

Bitcoin enters this conversation as a new kind of monetary system. To understand Bitcoin clearly, you first need to understand what money is trying to do in the first place. Bitcoin is not just "internet cash." It is an attempt to create money with strong monetary properties in a digital environment, without depending on a single issuer.

The key lesson is this: money is not valuable because paper, metal, or code is magical. Money is valuable because people trust it to coordinate exchange, measure value, and preserve purchasing power. Once you understand that, Bitcoin becomes much easier to evaluate.
